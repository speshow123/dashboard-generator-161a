				<h2><a href="http://ha.ckers.org/blog/20101201/and-beyond/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to And Beyond&#8230;">And Beyond&#8230;</a></h2>
				<small>December 1st, 2010 <!-- by RSnake --></small>
				
				<div class="entry">
					<p>Here we are, my friends.  The 1000th post.  Whew!  It&#8217;s quite a load off to have finally made it.  Hopefully this doesn&#8217;t come as a surprise to anyone since I&#8217;ve been announcing it for months, and if you have questions, hopefully the FAQ can answer them.  I wrote and re-wrote this post several times.  There&#8217;s so much to say.  How can you sum up 5 years of a blog in one post?  I have so much to say, but I&#8217;m not going to write a book about why I&#8217;m shutting the blog down, I&#8217;ll just focus on the major issue at hand - happiness.  Isn&#8217;t that what life&#8217;s really all about?</p>
<p>It wasn&#8217;t that long ago that I unfortunately lost my love affair with security.  Even a few years ago my wife would find me up way too late at night writing some little proof of concept code, excited to post about it the next day.  A lot has changed.  Some of it is external forces, and some of it is that I realized that I&#8217;ve done what I came here to do.  When I ask audiences at conferences how many people have heard of XSS or CSRF or command injection or SQL injection, nearly everyone raises their hand.  I can rest easy now in that the ultimate mission of the blog has been a success - people have been educated, partly through me, and partly because the industry at large has stepped up to the plate and done an amazing job of absorbing the problems.</p>
<p>I started ha.ckers.org as a place for me to experiment on my own, and share ideas with a few like-minded folks.  I never intended it to be a big site, but scope creep from the original mission changed all that.  I realized I could educate a lot more people than the 20 or so readers I had started out with.  20,000 readers, countless press articles and 5 years later, and I&#8217;ve been run through the meat grinder.  My love for security was unfortunately replaced by a sense of servitude.</p>
<p>With any kind of work you get a sense of anxiety.  But the biggest problem is that security stopped making me happy.  I got into security because I enjoyed the intellectual puzzle.  The industry around me has certainly changed several times since I got started but more importantly I too changed. My wife told me not too long ago that I wasn&#8217;t a hacker anymore, I was a politician, looking to see how I rated in the polls.  I really didn&#8217;t like what I had become - that&#8217;s not me at all.  I normally hate the press, and I&#8217;ve never enjoyed public speaking.  It was always a necessary evil.  An evil that I embraced far too much, if you ask me.</p>
<p>They say that if you look at the graph of happiness in your life you can tell what sort of life you led.  For instance, if your life starts positive, then goes down, and then ends positive it&#8217;s a comedy.  If it starts low, goes up and then ends badly, well, then you lead a tragic life.  I&#8217;ve never claimed to be a futurist, and in fact, I&#8217;ve found the question, &#8220;What do you think we&#8217;ll see in the future&#8221; to be a terrifying question - what if I&#8217;m completely wrong?</p>
<p>I&#8217;m not an oracle and I really don&#8217;t like giving people incorrect information.  But if I were to look at the graph of my life honestly, it wasn&#8217;t trending well over the last few years, looking more like downward trending saw blade of perpetual highs and lows.  Although there have been a lot of individual highlights and amazing things that have happened, I&#8217;ve noticed and other friends, family, and peers have noticed that I&#8217;ve gotten less and less happy as a whole.  As much as my trustworthy friends tried to convince me that the negative sentiment was meaningless, it was having a profound effect on my desire to continue.  The saw blade was trending downward.  I&#8217;m not blameless for how I got here - no one is perfect, least of all me.</p>
<p>Although I&#8217;m a fun loving person in many ways I also tend to be a pessimist and I do take things too seriously sometimes - definitely to a fault.  I saw my happiness declining and the light at the end of that tunnel was getting further and smaller as I went on.  It became harder to shrug things off, and I started worrying about even the simplest of things.  So instead of being a victim of my own circumstance I made a decision to make my own destiny and start enjoying life again.</p>
<p>So this is it - I&#8217;m taking my happiness back and I&#8217;ll be taking on new and exciting challenges without the drama of intense public scrutiny.  It&#8217;s time to make the graph of my life into a comedy - filled with excitement and wonder in the unknown.  I&#8217;ll always have a soft spot for security; I&#8217;ll keep up on it, and I&#8217;ll continue to research and run my company, among a lot of other things, insofar as it doesn&#8217;t impinge on my happiness.  Not hedonism, my friends, happiness.  Now is the time to seize the day and start having fun again.  Life&#8217;s too short.</p>
<p>I could also spend pages iterating all the people who&#8217;ve helped me think through the countless issues we&#8217;ve talked about, sent in ideas and generally made this website and WebAppSec in general a success.  Rather than risk excluding anyone all I can say is that I truly, deeply respect all of you for your skills and appreciate what you&#8217;ve done for me and the industry as a whole.  Perhaps no one but me will truly appreciate everything you&#8217;ve done, but trust me, you&#8217;re the real gods of WebAppSec.  I wish you the best.  So I leave it to you all - this industry along with all the good and bad, in very capable hands.  Trust me, there are plenty of amazing people out there.  Now it&#8217;s time for them to take their rightful place.</p>
<p>So&#8230; where can this mythical happiness monster be found, you may be asking?  For me the journey to find happiness starts with a cold beer - so that&#8217;s where I&#8217;m headed.  On behalf of id and myself, adios, my friends!  Thank you for reading.</p>

				<small>December 1st, 2010 <!-- by RSnake --></small>
				
				<div class="entry">
					<p>One post left&#8230;</p>
<p>I know people have a few questions about the remaining fate of the site, so I decided to write a little FAQ prior to my last post:</p>
<p><b>Q:  Are you planning on keeping ha.ckers.org up for reference at least?</b></p>
<p>Yes.  There&#8217;s a very small chance (read near zero) that I will be making any updates though.</p>
<p><b>Q:  Are you going to keep comments open on the blog?</b></p>
<p>The short answer is no.  I&#8217;ve already been shutting down comments on some of the older posts to reduce the volume of comment spam.  I&#8217;ll probably leave comments in place for a few months and then close it up, just to reduce the maintenance.  So if you have anything you want to say about any of the recent posts, please say it now.</p>
<p><b>Q:  Are you planning on keeping sla.ckers.org up?</b></p>
<p>For the foreseeable future, yes.  I do want to encourage people to keep researching, and innovating, even if I&#8217;m not directly a part of it.  So yes, there&#8217;s no plans on taking sla.ckers.org offline, and I still encourage people to visit and ask &#8220;dumb&#8221; questions.  You have to get started somewhere, and it all starts with intense curiosity.  For those who are starting, don&#8217;t be afraid to approach people who know what they&#8217;re talking about.  If they blow you off, they&#8217;re jerks, but a lot of times they&#8217;ll be patient and help.  It never hurts to try. <i>Update: sla.ckers.org and ha.ckers.org both suffered a massive RAID and simultaneous backup failure on December 17th 2010 related in part to an exhaust system failure in our redundant cooling system.  So some dates are messed up on comments over the last few months of posts, some files and directories (like hashmaster) are gone, and sla.ckers.org suffered some loss of posts because we had to go back to an old backup.  Sorry about that.  It&#8217;s hard to predict so many failures at once.</i> </p>
<p><b>Q:  I still want to read what you&#8217;re writing, are you posting anywhere else?</b></p>
<p>I may post in lots of places regarding various topics and for various reasons, but no, my days of WebAppSec blog posting a la ha.ckers.org are over.  It&#8217;s time for others to pick up where I left off.  But if you just want to read 300+ more pages of RSnake content, please check out <A HREF="http://www.detectmalice.com/">Detecting Malice</a>.</p>
<p><b>Q:  Why 1,000 posts and not 10,000 or 100,000 posts?</b></p>
<p>Because I made a promise to myself to make it to 1,000 posts.  That&#8217;s it.  Simple enough.  It was really easy to get to 100 posts, and even easier to get to 250.  After that, it got harder and harder.  I was thinking about stopping at 500, but one day I checked and I had accidentally gotten to over 550&#8230; so then I made another promise that I&#8217;d stop at 1000.  And here we are, my friends - one post remaining.</p>
<p><b>Q:  Someone mentioned to me something about a &#8220;Dread Pirate RSnake&#8221;.  What is that?</b></p>
<p>A year or so ago I was thinking that rather than shutting down the blog outright I would find a talented person to take my place.  Like the character in the Princess Bride, the Dread Pirate Roberts, they could take on the Dread Pirate RSnake persona, and pass that along to others once they got tired of the name.  I talked with several people about that who seemed interested in taking up the cause, but after thinking about it longer I decided it was a bad idea.  Ultimately I decided the blog was fun while it lasted, but it&#8217;s over for me, and my handle doesn&#8217;t need to live on.  The research is the important part and others have long ago taken over those reigns anyway.</p>
<p><b>Q:  Will you continue to be part of security?</b></p>
<p>In short, yes, I&#8217;ll still be working in security.  I&#8217;ll always be available by email, but no, my time in the spotlight is thankfully coming to a close.  It&#8217;s time for other people to get their moment in the sun.  Having already made a few commitments I will remain somewhat visibly involved in the security world, but otherwise I&#8217;m trying to do less and less in the public eye.  I&#8217;m definitely not leaving security altogether though.  SecTheory will continue to operate, and I have a number of security ideas in the works that will no doubt see the light of day at some point, but that&#8217;s about it.  And Jer seems to think I may twitter more now than ever.  Who knows?  Only time will tell.  I really dislike twittering though, so the forecast does not look good.</p>
<p><b>Q:  What about any other vulns you find?</b></p>
<p>Ah, the hardest question of all.  I haven&#8217;t made up my mind.  Some issues will no doubt get disclosed to the appropriate parties.  Some may end up in a friend&#8217;s lap for them to disclose under their name.  The remaining issues&#8230; who knows?  To be honest, like a lot of researchers these days, I&#8217;ll probably just sit on them.</p>

				<h2><a href="http://ha.ckers.org/blog/20101201/whats-left/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to What&#8217;s Left?">What&#8217;s Left?</a></h2>
				<small>December 1st, 2010 <!-- by RSnake --></small>
				
				<div class="entry">
					<p>2 posts left&#8230;</p>
<p>As I wind down, I&#8217;ve gotten a lot of requests to talk about various things in my final posts.  Everything from talking about what to study for newbies, how to keep up on WebAppSec when I&#8217;m gone, to talking about <a href="http://www.owasp.org/index.php/OWASP_O2_Platform">O2</a>.  But what I really want to talk about is what&#8217;s left?  After having researched for 15 years and having blogged for 5, what areas do I think are left to research/write/build?  There are tons of things.  I&#8217;ll just type free-form for the next few minutes:</p>
<p>- I think mobile browsers are Swiss cheese and they need a much more serious look.  And then we need to have a fierce conversation with the mobile providers about better/faster mechanisms to do patch management.</p>
<p>- I think browser port blocking blacklists are dumb and have already been broken at least three times.  It&#8217;s time to do a month of inter-protocol exploitation!</p>
<p>- I think browser UP&#038;P attacks against routers are highly likely and need a lot more research.</p>
<p>- I think the whole concept of replacing SSL/TLS with SSL/TLS over DNSSEC needs a ton of thought as a replacement.</p>
<p>- Browser UIs need to be hammered - they all have problems.</p>
<p>- Re-writing firmware in home DSL routers and making router-based botnets is under-researched.</p>
<p>- A table of all the ways to leak information across domains (img tags, style tags, iframes, etc&#8230;) needs to be kept and cataloged by browser type.</p>
<p>- An acid test should be built on a website somewhere so that people can test all known security problems against their browser.  Then we can start a healthy competition and track how long each browser takes to close each issue.</p>
<p>- Cloud providers need to be hacked to prove how frail everything is that rely on them.</p>
<p>- SSL/TLS resellers need to be hacked to prove how frail PKI is when you distribute it out to the least common denominator.</p>
<p>- Alternate encoding issues are still barely understood and very poorly documented.</p>
<p>- Someone needs to build a ubiquitous DoS (not DDoS) package that includes every known DoS tool and throw it into MetaSploit, so companies start having to test against it and start pressuring the vendors to fix the issues.</p>
<p>&#8230; and that&#8217;s just what I could type out in a few minutes.  Look, anyone who says there&#8217;s nothing left to research isn&#8217;t thinking creatively.  There&#8217;s an absolutely amazing amount of issues out there left to research, and projects to make the industry move faster.  One problem I wish the industry would get away from is saying something isn&#8217;t new or isn&#8217;t interesting.  If it&#8217;s not new but it&#8217;s still broken, there&#8217;s a problem there (Firesheep is a great example).  If you&#8217;re interested in it, don&#8217;t let other people tell you it&#8217;s not interesting.  Go ahead and research it!  So what&#8217;s left?  Everything&#8217;s left, my friends!  The world is yours!  You have the power to make amazing things happen if you so choose.  It&#8217;s just a matter of deciding what kind of world you want to live in.</p>

				<small>October 20th, 2010 <!-- by RSnake --></small>

					<p>11 more posts left&#8230;</p>
<p>While I was out at <a href="http://technet.microsoft.com/en-us/security/cc261637.aspx">Bluehat</a> I ended up having some good meetings between Intel, Mozilla and Adobe.  How are these companies related, you may ask?  Well all of them care about performance.  A year or so ago I was hanging out with the Intel guys and they informed me that they have a series of low level performance primitives that they surface through APIs.  At the time I wasn&#8217;t quite sure what to make of it.  Security and performance aren&#8217;t natural bedfellows - or at least I didn&#8217;t think so at the time.</p>
<p>I got to talking with both Microsoft and Mozilla last week about the need for default Adblocking software built into the browser.  Jeremiah thinks it should be opt-out and I think it should be opt-in, but either way, I think we&#8217;re coming to a consensus that it should be automatically part of the browser in some form.  Mozilla was the first to give me a real reason it may be a problem other than it hurting Google, who is their biggest sponsor.  The reason is performance.  Adblockplus, as an example uses partial string regex which is a performance hog.  To put that in the browser by default would really make people&#8217;s experience suffer.  Then it occurred to me that I had had a conversation about performance with Intel a year before.  The answer, my friends, lies in primitives.</p>
<p>Currently Intel supports a subset of basic math functions and Perl&#8217;s version of regex.  Well, in a future version the chips could support things like the JavaScript version of regex, and other primitives involved in decision making and image/vector rendering and so on that are used within the browser.  Adobe is in the same boat - although probably a different subset of primitives would be desirable.  Then the idea sprang up to use these primitives within Visual Studio itself to get more generic/native improvements to performance without developers having to know anything about the chip.  Intel doesn&#8217;t tend to market these concepts very well, despite how interesting they could be, but only a few people have to know to make a big difference.</p>
<p>So now the real question isn&#8217;t whether these companies will pick up on this technology now that they know about it - that&#8217;s a given.  The real question then is once they get a performance boost are they going to use some of it to improve security or are they just going to tout themselves as the fastest?  At some point we have to stop and ask ourselves how fast do we really have to get before we start using some of that processing power to make people safer instead?  One can only hope&#8230;</p>
